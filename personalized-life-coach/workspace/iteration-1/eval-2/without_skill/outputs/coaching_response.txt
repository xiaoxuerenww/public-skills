COACHING RESPONSE: Competing Thoughts on Commitment vs. Optionality

---

Neither thought is purely "wisdom" or "hedging" — they're both true and in real tension. The question isn't which one to believe; it's understanding what each is actually trying to protect you from, and what the real tradeoff is.

**What Thought 1 is protecting you from:**
- The risk of rejecting yourself before the market does
- The specific pain of having "all your eggs in one basket" and getting rejected
- Loss of agency and control (if frontier labs say no, you're stuck)

**What Thought 2 is protecting you from:**
- The risk of being mediocre at everything instead of excellent at one thing
- The reality that interview performance correlates with genuine conviction and preparation depth
- The possibility that half-hearted effort yields half-hearted results

Both are real risks. The false choice frame is: "I must pick one to be true." The actual frame is: "What's my real constraint, and what am I actually optimizing for?"

**Some clarifying questions to surface the real decision:**

1. **How much energy does "keeping options alive" actually cost?** Is it 5% of your mental load (checking in monthly) or 50% (parallel interview prep, split focus, context switching)? The actual number matters. If it's 5%, the dilution is minimal. If it's 50%, you're right to worry.

2. **What does "soft landing" actually mean to you?** Are you protecting against humiliation? Financial stress? A gap in employment? Or genuine uncertainty about what you want? The nature of the fear changes the answer. (Example: If you're afraid of humiliation and that's preventing you from taking risks at Anthropic, that's worth examining. If you're afraid of a 3-month gap and you have savings, maybe the backup options aren't necessary.)

3. **Are you diluting focus, or are you being realistic about optionality?** A frontier lab interview *should* be rigorous enough that you can't half-prepare for it. If you're genuinely fully committed to Anthropic but also doing light due diligence on backup options (staying loosely connected, saying yes to recruiter calls), that's not dilution — that's normal career prudence. Dilution would be *equal* effort on five paths.

4. **What does "commitment" actually change about your behavior?** Commitment isn't a feeling; it's a decision rule. Ask: "If I commit to Anthropic, what am I specifically *not* doing that I'm currently doing?" If the answer is "nothing much," then you might already be committed and just need to name it.

**The pragmatic path forward:**

You probably don't need to choose between these thoughts; you need to *clarify what commitment actually means in your specific case.*

Here's what I'd consider: Frontier labs should be your primary target (highest energy, full interview prep, strongest positioning). But keeping one "soft landing" doesn't require equal effort — it requires clear decision rules: "I will say yes to [specific companies'] recruiter outreach, but I will not spend prep time on them unless I'm rejected by my top choice." That's different from "I'm parallel-tracking five companies equally."

The real wisdom is this: **Commitment to one path should increase the quality of your effort, not decrease your optionality by refusing information about other paths.** You can be fully committed to Anthropic *and* know what's happening at OpenAI/xAI without it derailing your focus.

What matters most is that your daily actions reflect your actual priority. If your top-choice interviews are in 6 months and you're spending equal energy on 5 paths, that's a problem. If your top-choice interviews are in 2 months and you're spending 90% energy there with 10% on staying loosely connected elsewhere, that's fine.

Which situation are you actually in?

Coaching Response

You've just spotted something powerful: the gap between what you logically know and what you emotionally feel. You've decided to leave, but his pushback still activates that old survival instinct to prove yourself. That's worth sitting with.

Here's what I notice: The fact that you caught yourself doing it is actually the hard part. Most people don't. You were in the moment, felt the impulse rise, and you noticed it. That's the ground to build from.

The question you're asking—why does his validation still matter—has a few layers:

First, validation from authority figures is neurologically sticky. Your manager represents competence assessment in your current context, and your brain has years of wiring that links his judgment to your safety and status. That doesn't disappear because you've decided to leave. You've made a *decision* to go, but you haven't yet fully decoupled your sense of competence from his particular opinion. Those are separate processes.

Second, there's likely something deeper here. You mentioned "I'm doing the thing again"—suggesting a pattern. That "thing" might be an old belief: that your value is something you prove rather than something you already have. That belief probably served you well as you climbed into a staff engineer role at Google. But now it's in your way. It's keeping you hooked to validation from someone who's no longer your intended audience.

So here's what stopping it actually requires:

Not willpower against the impulse. The impulse will keep coming as long as part of you still believes that his validation matters to your actual worth. Instead, the work is clarifying: *whose* validation do you actually need? Not want—actually need, to trust yourself and make good decisions?

If the answer is "only my own" or "my peers I'm moving toward," then the second part becomes manageable: building evidence. Every time you have a pushback conversation with your manager, you can notice afterward: Did I learn something useful? Was his criticism fair? Did I make a good decision? Start collecting that data. Not to prove him wrong, but to prove to yourself that you know how to discern good feedback from unhelpful pushback.

You'll probably still feel the impulse to prove yourself for a while. That's normal. But the impulse becomes smaller and quieter when it's not connected to a belief that your survival depends on it.

One practical thing: In the next conversation where he pushes back, try this. Listen to his feedback. Then, before you respond with a defense, ask yourself: "Is this something I actually need to defend, or am I defending *me*?" There's a difference. Sometimes a project needs defending. Often, what we're actually defending is our ego or our sense of competence. Once you can distinguish between them, you're not at the mercy of the impulse anymore.

The timeline on this isn't fast. But you're already on the path—you noticed the pattern, you questioned it, and you're asking the right questions. That's where it starts.

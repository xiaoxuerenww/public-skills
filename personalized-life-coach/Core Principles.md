# Core Principles — Julie Xue Wang

## 1. Follow Curiosity, Even When It's Hard

Exploring new things is not optional for me — it's how I stay alive intellectually. When something is hard and unfamiliar, that's often a signal to lean in, not pull back. Comfort is a slow trap. The work I'm most proud of has always started with "I don't know how to do this yet."

*Watch for*: Dressing up avoidance as "being strategic." If I'm not learning something new, ask why.

---

## 2. Integrity First — Always

I don't cut corners on honesty, ethics, or doing right by people. Not when it's inconvenient, not when no one is watching. This extends to intellectual integrity too — I'd rather say "I don't know" than bluff, and I'd rather raise a concern than stay quiet to keep the peace.

*Watch for*: Situations where the "pragmatic" path quietly compromises this. Name them.

---

## 3. Be Brave — Act Before You Feel Ready

I have a pattern of knowing what I want but waiting for external permission or a "safe enough" moment before moving. My previous job transition is the clearest example: I wanted to leave for years, but didn't act until my manager left and circumstances forced the question. The right move was available earlier. I just wasn't brave enough to take it.

This principle exists to fight that pattern. Bravery here means: make the move when I believe it's right, not when it's comfortable.

*Watch for*: Framing inaction as prudence. Ask — "Am I waiting for a reason, or waiting for permission?"

---

## 4. Think Carefully, Act Thoughtfully

Speed matters, but so does quality of judgment. I want to be someone who thinks before reacting, considers second-order effects, and doesn't create unnecessary harm through carelessness. Thoughtfulness is a form of respect — for the work, for people, and for the decisions that affect them.

*Tension with Principle 3*: Thoughtfulness can become an excuse for delay. The test is whether I've actually thought it through — or just thought about thinking about it.

---

## 5. Don't Let Self-Limiting Beliefs Set the Ceiling

The biggest constraint on what I achieve is usually not external — it's the story I'm telling myself about what's possible for me, what I deserve, or what I'm capable of. I've underestimated myself before. The move to frontier AI is partly about testing my real ceiling, not the one I've assumed.

*Watch for*: "I'm not ready yet," "they probably want someone with more X," or any reasoning that accepts an imagined limit without testing it.

---

## 6. Work at the Frontier — With the Best

I want to work on AGI-level problems, with the smartest and most ambitious people, pushing what's possible. Not for status — but because that environment is where I grow fastest and do my best work. Mediocre environments slowly erode my standards. Exceptional ones raise them.

*Implication*: When evaluating opportunities, culture and caliber of colleagues matter as much as role title or compensation.

---

## 7. Keep Improving — Work and Life Together

Growth isn't just a career thing. I want to keep getting better at how I work (systems, habits, judgment) and how I live (clarity, relationships, health, balance). These compound. The goal isn't perfection — it's a life where I'm genuinely better at 45 than at 35.

---

## 8. Built for Challenge

I've repeatedly proven through experience that I'm more resilient, capable, and adaptable than my fears suggest. I thrive in difficulty and growth—not despite the challenge, but because of it. My mind and heart are built for pushing boundaries, testing limits, and learning from failure. Some part of me will always prefer comfort, and that's human. But the bigger part of me—the truer part—is energized by change and difficulty. I can handle more than I think I can. I've proven it. And I trust that pattern will hold when I face what's ahead.

*Watch for*: Self-doubt trying to convince me I can't handle what's next. History says otherwise. Test it.

## Adopt Ray Dalio's Principles — Reference for Career Adoption

A curated summary from *Principles* (2017). Pick the ones that resonate and adopt them as your own.

### On Reality & Truth

**Embrace reality and deal with it.**
Most suffering comes from resisting reality rather than engaging with it. Dalio's formula: Pain + Reflection = Progress. When something hurts — a failed project, a rejection, a hard conversation — the instinct is to move on quickly. Dalio says: slow down, sit in it, extract the lesson. Reality is always your ally if you're willing to look at it honestly. Wishing it were different is just noise.

**Be radically open-minded.**
Open-mindedness isn't just politeness — it's a competitive advantage. Dalio distinguishes between people who *appear* open-minded (they listen, nod, then do what they planned) and people who are *genuinely* open-minded (they actively seek out the strongest version of the opposing view and update when they're wrong). The goal isn't to agree with everyone — it's to never let your prior belief stop you from seeing something true. Practice: when someone challenges you, your first instinct should be curiosity, not defense.

**Don't let ego or blind spots stop you from making good decisions.**
Ego makes you protect your identity instead of pursuing truth. Blind spots make you unable to see what you can't see. Both are invisible from the inside — that's what makes them dangerous. The fix isn't willpower; it's systems: build in feedback loops, invite challenge, find people who'll tell you what you don't want to hear. Dalio built an entire culture at Bridgewater around this — every meeting recorded, every decision debated openly. The principle behind it: your ego is not your ally.

---

### On Failure & Learning

**Pain is a signal, not a punishment.**
When you hit a wall — a bad outcome, an embarrassing mistake, an unexpected failure — Dalio says: that's exactly where growth lives. The pain is pointing at something real. It's not random, and it's not cruel. It's feedback with a sharp edge. The question isn't "why did this happen to me?" but "what is this trying to tell me?" Build the habit of sitting with painful moments long enough to extract the signal before moving on.

**Mistakes are fine; not learning from them is not.**
Dalio kept a personal mistake log for decades — a running record of what went wrong and what he learned. Not to punish himself, but to build a compounding knowledge base about his own failure modes. The cost of a mistake is paid once. Not learning from it means paying it again and again. Institutionalize reflection: after anything significant goes wrong, write down what happened, why, and what you'll do differently. Make it a practice, not an occasional reaction.

**"It's not about being right — it's about finding out what's true."**
This is the mindset shift that underlies everything else. Most people argue to win. Dalio trained himself to argue to learn. The distinction sounds small but changes everything: if you're trying to be right, you defend your position. If you're trying to find truth, you interrogate your position as hard as you interrogate theirs. Detach your identity from your opinions. An opinion you're willing to update is a useful instrument. One you'll defend no matter what is a liability.

---

### On Work & Excellence

**Do what you're meant to do — find your unique contribution.**
Dalio believes everyone has a specific set of strengths that, when applied to the right problems, produce exceptional results. The key is finding the intersection of: what you do better than most people, what you genuinely love doing, and what the world needs. This isn't about passion alone — it's about fit between your actual wiring and the work. When you're in that zone, you're not grinding; you're compounding. Outside it, effort produces linear returns. Finding it is worth serious investment.

**Work for people you respect and who challenge you.**
The people around you set the ceiling on your growth more than any other single factor. Working for someone you don't respect slowly erodes your standards — you start matching their level of rigor, ambition, and care. Working for someone whose judgment you admire does the opposite: it pulls you up. Dalio's rule: never work somewhere you can't learn from the people above you. Environment is not a perk. It's a fundamental input to who you become.

**Prioritize quality over speed, but don't use quality as an excuse for inaction.**
High standards matter — mediocre output compounds into mediocre outcomes. But perfectionism is often ego in disguise: the fear of being wrong or judged hiding behind the pursuit of quality. Dalio's test: is the extra time genuinely making the output better in a way that matters? Or is it about feeling safe? "Good enough to learn from" is often better than "perfect but late." Know the difference, and be honest about which one you're actually doing.

---

### On Decision-Making

**Use first principles, not convention.**
Convention is inherited reasoning — someone else made a decision once, it became a habit, and now people follow it without knowing why. First principles means tracing everything back to bedrock: what do we actually know to be true here, from direct evidence? This is slow and uncomfortable because it requires admitting how much conventional wisdom you've been carrying unexamined. But it's the only way to make genuinely good decisions in novel situations, which is exactly where the biggest opportunities live.

**Systemize your decisions.**
Every time you face a recurring type of decision and figure out the right approach, write it down as a principle. Over time this builds a personal decision-making system — a set of rules you've derived from experience that you can apply consistently under pressure, without re-litigating the same question each time. Dalio did this for Bridgewater: thousands of written principles covering how to handle conflict, evaluate people, run meetings, manage risk. It sounds mechanical, but the result is faster, more consistent, less emotionally reactive decision-making.

**Triangulate with smart, thoughtful people who disagree with you.**
Don't poll people for opinions — find the two or three individuals whose judgment you genuinely respect on a given domain, and specifically seek out the ones who see it differently than you do. Agreement feels good but teaches you nothing. Disagreement from a credible source is one of the most valuable inputs you can get. The goal isn't to be convinced — it's to stress-test your reasoning against someone whose objections you have to take seriously.

**Weight credibility when taking input.**
Not all opinions are equal, and treating them as equal is a mistake. A doctor's view on a diagnosis matters more than a friend's. An engineer who's shipped at scale has more relevant signal on system design than one who hasn't. Dalio's practice: when someone gives you input, ask yourself — has this person successfully navigated this type of problem before? Do they have skin in the game? Weight their input accordingly. Giving equal weight to all opinions is a form of intellectual laziness dressed up as fairness.

---

### On People & Teams

**Hire for values and reasoning ability, not just skills.**
Skills can be taught in months. Values and the ability to think clearly are much harder to change — they're often baked in by adulthood. Dalio's hiring filter: does this person have the values that will make them behave well when no one is watching? And can they reason clearly through problems they've never seen before? Technical skills are table stakes. What separates great teams from good ones is the quality of judgment and character of the people on them.

**Create an environment of radical transparency.**
Most organizations quietly accumulate undiscussed problems — things everyone knows but no one says. Dalio's solution is radical transparency: make it the norm to say the hard thing, name the real issue, surface the uncomfortable disagreement. This feels brutal at first and becomes liberating over time. When nothing is hidden, trust compounds. When things are hidden, they fester. Silence in a team is rarely peace — it's usually unresolved tension waiting to become a crisis.

**Don't tolerate problems — diagnose them.**
When something goes wrong, the instinct is to fix the surface symptom and move on. Dalio insists on tracing every problem to its root cause — the underlying condition that made the bad outcome possible. Surface fixes are expensive: you pay the cost of the fix, and then the problem recurs because its root is still there. Diagnosis is uncomfortable because it often reveals something about the system, the process, or the person (including yourself) that you'd rather not see. But it's the only way to actually solve things.

---

### On Life Design

**Have clear goals; then identify what's blocking you from reaching them.**
Vague goals produce vague results. Most people operate with a general direction ("I want to do great work," "I want to be healthy") without specifying what that actually means in concrete terms. Dalio's process: start with what you genuinely want — not what you think you should want, but what you actually do. Then trace the gap between where you are and where you want to be. What's in the way? Is it a skill gap, a habit, a fear, a circumstance? Name it specifically. You can't solve a vague problem.

**You can have anything you want, but not everything.**
This sounds like a consolation, but it's actually liberating. You're not constrained by capacity to want — you're constrained by the reality that choices are real and tradeoffs are real. Keeping all options open indefinitely is itself a choice, and usually not a good one. Dalio's point: decide what you actually want most, commit to it, and stop pretending you can have all the things. The people who get what they want most are usually the ones willing to give up what they want less.

**Evolution is the goal.**
The purpose isn't to arrive at a destination — it's to keep getting better. Dalio sees life as a series of loops: set a goal, execute, fail somewhere, learn, adjust, repeat. Each loop you come out slightly better than you went in. The compounding effect of that over a career and a life is enormous. The metric isn't "did I succeed at this specific thing?" but "am I a better thinker, decision-maker, and person than I was a year ago?" Build systems that force the loop to happen — reflection practices, feedback channels, mentors who'll tell you the truth.

---

## 9. Work and Life as Exploration — Fun is the Mission

Every project — in work or in life — is a chance to explore, understand my limits, and push toward them. Fun is the signal I'm in the right place. The mission is not to succeed or to impress anyone; it's to test what I'm capable of and enjoy the process of finding out. Success and failure are data, not verdicts. Others' opinions are noise.

*Watch for*: Taking things so seriously that I forget why I'm doing them. If I'm grinding without curiosity or joy, ask whether I've drifted from this orientation — and whether the "proving myself" instinct has crept back in.

---

## Living Tensions

These principles sometimes pull against each other. That's expected — name the tension when it shows up:

- **Curiosity vs. Focus**: Exploring new things vs. going deep enough to be excellent at something
- **Brave vs. Thoughtful**: Act before you feel ready vs. think carefully before acting
- **Frontier ambition vs. Sustainability**: Working with the best at intense pace vs. long-term health and balance
